# Oral Pathobiont Translocation and Microglial Activation Under Chronic Temenon Exposure: Evidence for a Gut/Oral-Brain Inflammatory Axis

## ABSTRACT

**Background:** Ramirez et al. (2163) demonstrated that chronic Temenon field exposure produces host-microbiome eigentime desynchronization, resulting in progressive gut and oral dysbiosis mediated by differential bacterial replication kinetics. This dysbiosis statistically explained a substantial proportion of previously unexplained stress phenotypes in Temenon-exposed animals. Whether this peripheral inflammatory state translates into measurable central nervous system consequences—and whether recently characterized brain-resident immune cell populations are implicated—remained untested.

**Objective:** To determine whether chronic Temenon-associated dysbiosis produces microglial activation, oral pathobiont translocation to CNS tissue, and early markers of neurodegenerative pathology, and to characterize the novel perivascular immune cell population implicated in these processes.

**Methods:** Archived and newly generated brain tissue from Temenon-exposed mice (Chen et al. 2159 and Ramirez et al. 2163 cohorts, β=0-0.7, plus a new 180-day extended-exposure cohort) was analyzed for microglial density and activation state (Iba1, CD68 immunohistochemistry), perivascular immune cell populations (spectral flow cytometry, 28-parameter panel), bacterial DNA presence in brain tissue (16S qPCR, species-specific PCR for oral pathobionts), and early neurodegenerative markers (phosphorylated tau, amyloid-β oligomers, α-synuclein aggregates). Germ-free and antibiotic-suppressed-replication cohorts from Ramirez et al. served as mechanistic comparators.

**Results:** Chronic Temenon exposure (β=0.7, 90 days) produced significant microglial activation in hippocampus and amygdala (Iba1+ density +52%, amoeboid morphology +41%, p<0.001), which was substantially attenuated in replication-suppressed and germ-free comparator cohorts (attenuation 58-71%). *Porphyromonas gingivalis*-specific DNA was detected in brain tissue of 34% of β=0.7 animals versus 3% of controls (p<0.001), localizing preferentially to hippocampal and periventricular regions. We identified an expanded population of CD11b+CD45^int Ly6C^low perivascular cells—phenotypically consistent with recently described disease-associated perivascular macrophage-like cells—that correlated strongly with both bacterial translocation burden (r=0.67) and phosphorylated tau accumulation (r=0.58) at 180 days. Extended exposure (180 days) revealed early but significant elevation of hippocampal phosphorylated tau (+34%, p=0.003) and α-synuclein oligomers in substantia nigra-adjacent tissue (+28%, p=0.014), the latter finding of particular note given independent evidence linking oral pathobiont burden to Parkinsonian pathology via analogous translocation routes.

**Conclusions:** We provide direct mechanistic evidence linking Temenon-induced dysbiosis to measurable CNS neuroinflammatory and early neurodegenerative changes via a translocation-dependent pathway involving a specific perivascular immune cell population. These findings substantially raise the safety concerns associated with chronic Temenon exposure, particularly for long-duration applications, and identify a novel oral-brain axis with direct relevance to iatrogenic neurodegenerative risk.

**Keywords:** Temenon field, microglia, neuroinflammation, oral-brain axis, *Porphyromonas gingivalis*, perivascular macrophages, tau pathology, α-synuclein, dysbiosis

## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 From Dysbiosis to the Brain: An Open Question

Ramirez et al. (2163) established that prokaryotic organisms fail to couple to Temenon eigentime fields, producing progressive host-microbiome desynchronization under chronic exposure [1]. This mechanistic insight resolved a substantial portion (approximately 58%) of the previously unexplained stress and anxiety phenotype reported in Chen et al.'s foundational validation study [2], via gut barrier compromise, endotoxemia (elevated LPS-binding protein), and downstream corticosterone elevation.

However, two critical questions remained unaddressed by that work:

1. **Does peripheral dysbiosis-associated inflammation translate into measurable CNS pathology?** Systemic inflammation and gut-derived endotoxemia are independently associated with neuroinflammatory priming in the aging and disease literature [3,4], but this had not been directly tested in the Temenon exposure paradigm.

2. **Is the oral microbiome shift identified by Ramirez et al.—specifically the marked expansion of *Porphyromonas* and *Fusobacterium* species [1]—of particular concern given independent evidence linking these specific pathobionts to neurodegenerative processes?**

### 1.2 The Oral-Brain Axis: Independent Evidence Base

Recent literature has substantially strengthened the case for a mechanistic connection between periodontal pathobiont burden and neurodegenerative disease. Weber & Fischer (2162) reviewed converging evidence that *Porphyromonas gingivalis* and its virulence factors (notably gingipains) can translocate from oral tissue to brain parenchyma, where they are associated with neuroinflammatory cascades and tau hyperphosphorylation [5]. Independently, everyday oral hygiene behaviors have been highlighted as an underappreciated modifiable risk factor in Parkinsonian pathology, with mechanical disruption of gingival tissue during toothbrushing proposed as a potential facilitator of bacterial and inflammatory mediator translocation into systemic and possibly CNS circulation [6].

Separately, newly characterized populations of brain-resident and perivascular immune cells have been implicated in age-related neurodegenerative processes, with evidence suggesting these populations respond dynamically to peripheral inflammatory state and may serve as an early, modifiable node in neurodegenerative cascades rather than a fixed structural feature of the aging brain [7].

**No prior study has connected these two independent literatures to Temenon biology.** Given that Ramirez et al. specifically documented oral *Porphyromonas* expansion (+3.6-fold at β=0.7) [1]—the identical pathobiont implicated in the oral-brain axis literature—we considered this connection a high-priority, directly testable hypothesis.

### 1.3 Study Objectives

1. Determine whether chronic Temenon exposure produces measurable microglial activation in brain regions relevant to memory and movement (hippocampus, amygdala, substantia nigra-adjacent tissue)
2. Test for direct bacterial DNA translocation to brain tissue, with specific attention to *P. gingivalis*
3. Characterize perivascular immune cell populations potentially mediating translocation-associated neuroinflammation
4. Assess whether replication-suppressed or germ-free conditions (which attenuate peripheral dysbiosis per Ramirez et al.) correspondingly attenuate CNS findings, providing mechanistic linkage
5. Screen for early markers of tau and α-synuclein pathology under extended (180-day) exposure

## 2. METHODS

### 2.1 Ethical Approval

Approved by Luna Animal Care Committee (LACC-2162-091) and Heidelberg Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (HD-IACUC-2162-034), in continuation of protocols established under LACC-2156-042 and LACC-2161-078.

### 2.2 Animal Cohorts

**Cohort A (Archived tissue, 90-day):** Brain tissue from the Chen et al. (2159) and Ramirez et al. (2163) cohorts, previously collected but not subjected to neuroimmune analysis. Includes Control, β=0.3, 0.5, 0.7 groups (n=48-60 per group) and Ramirez et al.'s gnotobiotic comparator groups (Live+β=0.7, Irradiated+β=0.7, Antibiotic-suppressed+β=0.7; n=20 each).

**Cohort B (New, 180-day extended exposure):** C57BL/6J mice (n=96), conventionally raised, randomized to Control (β=0) or β=0.7, exposed continuously for 180 days (approximately 14 human-equivalent years [8]) to assess whether early tau/synuclein changes emerge with extended duration beyond the original 90-day protocol.

### 2.3 Neuroimmune and Neuropathological Assessment

**Microglial density and morphology:**
- Immunohistochemistry: Iba1 (pan-microglial marker), CD68 (activation marker)
- Regions: hippocampus (CA1, CA3, dentate gyrus), basolateral amygdala, substantia nigra pars compacta and adjacent striatum
- Morphological classification: ramified (surveillant) vs. amoeboid (activated), automated via Fiji/ImageJ skeletal analysis [9]
- Quantification: cells/mm², blinded to group assignment

**Perivascular immune cell characterization:**
- Spectral flow cytometry (Cytek Aurora, 28-parameter panel)
- Brain dissociation via enzymatic digestion + Percoll gradient (myelin removal)
- Markers: CD45, CD11b, Ly6C, Ly6G, CX3CR1, CD206, MHC-II, CD68, P2RY12, TMEM119 (core microglial identity markers to distinguish resident microglia from infiltrating/perivascular populations)
- Population of interest: CD11b+CD45^int Ly6C^low cells, phenotypically distinct from both resident P2RY12^hi microglia and Ly6C^hi infiltrating monocytes, hypothesized as analogous to recently described disease-associated perivascular populations [7]

**Bacterial translocation assessment:**
- Total bacterial 16S qPCR (brain tissue, cardiac-perfused to exclude vascular contamination)
- Species-specific qPCR: *P. gingivalis* (fimA gene), *Fusobacterium nucleatum*
- Confirmatory: fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) with *P. gingivalis*-specific probe in a subset (n=15 per group)

**Neurodegenerative pathology markers:**
- Phosphorylated tau (AT8 epitope, pSer202/pThr205): ELISA (hippocampal homogenate) + immunohistochemistry
- Amyloid-β oligomers: ELISA (Aβ42-specific)
- α-synuclein: total and oligomeric forms, ELISA (substantia nigra-adjacent tissue homogenate)

### 2.4 Behavioral Correlation

Elevated Plus Maze and open field data from the original Chen et al. and Ramirez et al. protocols [1,2] were re-analyzed for correlation with newly generated neuroimmune data in the same animals (archived Cohort A) where tissue permitted paired analysis.

### 2.5 Statistical Analysis

**Software:** R v4.9.0 (packages: lme4, FlowSOM, ggplot2, mediation)

**Group comparisons:** Mixed-effects models with cohort/batch as random effect, Tukey HSD post-hoc with FDR correction

**Mediation analysis:** Sequential mediation testing pathway: β (Temenon exposure) → dysbiosis (Ramirez et al. markers) → bacterial translocation → perivascular cell expansion → microglial activation → tau/synuclein pathology

**Pre-registration:** IPTR-2162-0678

### 3.3 Bacterial DNA Translocation to Brain Tissue

**Table 3. Bacterial DNA Detection in Perfused Brain Tissue (Day 90)**

| Group | n | Total 16S+ (%) | *P. gingivalis*+ (%) | *F. nucleatum*+ (%) |
|-------|---|-------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Control | 60 | 5.0 | 3.3 | 1.7 |

*p<0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001 vs. Control (Fisher's exact test)

*P. gingivalis*-specific DNA was detected in brain tissue of 33% of β=0.7 animals, an order of magnitude above control background (3.3%, likely representing minor perfusion artifact or trace environmental contamination). FISH confirmation (subset, n=15/group) validated qPCR findings, with visible *P. gingivalis* signal localizing predominantly to periventricular and hippocampal perivascular spaces, consistent with a blood-brain-barrier-adjacent translocation route rather than diffuse parenchymal invasion.

**Regional distribution:** Bacterial DNA burden correlated with regional microglial activation (Spearman r=0.71, p<0.001, hippocampus), supporting a spatially coherent translocation-inflammation relationship.

### 3.4 A Novel Perivascular Immune Cell Population Links Translocation to Neuroinflammation

Spectral flow cytometry identified a CD11b+CD45^int Ly6C^low P2RY12^low CX3CR1^int population, phenotypically distinct from both resident microglia (P2RY12^hi, TMEM119^hi) and classical infiltrating monocytes (Ly6C^hi), which we term **Translocation-Associated Perivascular Cells (TAPCs)** for descriptive purposes pending further phenotypic characterization.

|---------|---------|-------|-----------------------------------|
| TAPC frequency (% CD45+ cells) | 2.1±0.9 | 8.7±3.2*** | — |

TAPC frequency was 4.1-fold higher in β=0.7 animals at Day 180 and correlated strongly with bacterial translocation burden, tau phosphorylation, and behavioral anxiety phenotype. We interpret TAPCs as a plausible cellular intermediary linking peripheral bacterial translocation to local neuroinflammatory and early proteinopathic changes, consistent in general phenotype with recently described disease-associated perivascular macrophage populations implicated in aging-related neurodegeneration [7], though we emphasize this identification is provisional pending single-cell transcriptomic characterization (see Limitations).

### 3.5 Extended Exposure (180 Days) Reveals Early Tau and Synuclein Pathology

**Table 5. Neurodegenerative Markers at Extended Exposure Duration**

| Marker | Region | Control (Day 180) | β=0.7 (Day 180) | % Change | p |
|--------|--------|---------------------|---------------------|-----------|---|
| Phosphorylated tau (AT8) | Hippocampus | 79±21 pg/mg | 106±31 pg/mg | +34% | 0.003 |
| Amyloid-β42 oligomers | Hippocampus | 18±6 pg/mg | 22±8 pg/mg | +22% | 0.089 (n.s.) |
| α-synuclein oligomers | SN-adjacent | 3.9±1.6 ng/mg | 5.0±2.0 ng/mg | +28% | 0.014 |

At 180 days, we observed statistically significant elevation of phosphorylated tau (hippocampus) and oligomeric α-synuclein (substantia nigra-adjacent tissue), while total amyloid-β and total α-synuclein levels remained unchanged. This pattern—selective elevation of pathological oligomeric/phosphorylated species without corresponding change in total protein levels—is consistent with an early, inflammation-associated proteinopathic process rather than a generalized neurodegenerative state, and should be interpreted as a preliminary signal warranting longer-duration follow-up rather than evidence of overt disease.

**We emphasize that these animals showed no gross neurological deficits, and these findings represent subclinical, early biochemical changes**, not established neurodegenerative disease.

### 3.6 Sequential Mediation Analysis

We tested the full proposed causal chain using sequential mediation analysis:

**β (Temenon) → Dysbiosis (Shannon diversity, from Ramirez et al.) → Bacterial CNS translocation → TAPC expansion → Microglial activation → p-tau elevation**

**Table 6. Mediation Pathway Analysis (Day 180, Cohort B subset with paired peripheral+CNS data, n=42)**

| Pathway segment | Proportion mediated | 95% CI |
|--------------------|------------------------|--------|
| β → Dysbiosis → Translocation | 74% | 61-85% |
| Translocation → TAPC → Microglial activation | 68% | 52-79% |
| Microglial activation → p-tau | 41% | 24-58% |
| **Full pathway (β → p-tau)** | **31%** | **19-46%** |

Approximately one-third of the total effect of Temenon exposure on hippocampal tau phosphorylation was statistically explained by the full proposed pathway, with the remainder presumably reflecting additional mechanisms (direct CNS eigentime effects, as hypothesized but not confirmed by Chen et al. [2], or pathways not captured in our model). This partial mediation indicates the oral-gut-brain translocation axis is a substantial but not exclusive contributor to Temenon-associated neuropathological risk.

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Principal Findings

This study extends the mechanistic chain established by Chen et al. [2] and Ramirez et al. [1] into the CNS, demonstrating that:

1. Chronic Temenon exposure produces significant, region-specific microglial activation
2. This activation is substantially (58-71%) dependent on bacterial replication-driven dysbiosis, as shown by attenuation in replication-suppressed comparator cohorts
3. Oral pathobiont DNA, particularly *P. gingivalis*, translocates to brain tissue in a dose- and exposure-dependent manner
4. A novel perivascular immune cell population (TAPCs) correlates strongly with translocation burden and early tau pathology
5. Extended (180-day) exposure produces measurable, if subclinical, elevation in phosphorylated tau and oligomeric α-synuclein

### 4.2 Completing the Mechanistic Arc

Three papers now form a coherent causal narrative:

**Chen et al. (2159):** Temenon works, but produces unexplained stress phenotype
**Ramirez et al. (2163):** Stress substantially explained by microbiome desynchronization (host couples, bacteria don't)
**This study (2164):** Microbiome disruption translocates to brain, activates a specific immune population, and produces early proteinopathic changes

This progression illustrates a broader principle for Temenon safety science: **effects that appear neurological or psychiatric in origin may in fact be peripherally initiated and mechanistically tractable**, with direct implications for intervention strategy (see Section 4.4).

### 4.3 The TAPC Population: Provisional Interpretation

We identify TAPCs descriptively based on surface phenotype and functional correlation, not through definitive lineage tracing or single-cell transcriptomics. Their phenotype (CD45^int, Ly6C^low, P2RY12^low) suggests a population distinct from both resident microglia and classical monocyte-derived infiltrates—potentially representing a perivascular macrophage subset recruited or activated specifically in response to translocated bacterial material, analogous in general character to recently characterized disease-associated perivascular populations in aging-related neuroinflammatory contexts [7]. We deliberately avoid over-interpreting this population's identity or function beyond what our data support, and flag single-cell RNA sequencing and fate-mapping studies as essential next steps (Section 4.6).

### 4.4 Implications for the Oral-Brain Axis Literature

Our findings provide direct experimental support, in a controlled model system, for a mechanistic pathway that has been proposed but difficult to establish causally in human epidemiological work: oral pathobiont burden → systemic translocation → CNS immune activation → early proteinopathy. The specific implication of *P. gingivalis*, previously highlighted as a candidate contributor to neurodegenerative processes via gingipain-mediated tissue damage and translocation [5], and the parallel with concerns regarding routine oral hygiene practices and mechanical facilitation of bacterial translocation [6], is notable. Our data suggest that **any condition producing chronic oral dysbiosis with pathobiont expansion**—of which Temenon exposure is one example—could plausibly engage this same pathway.

### 4.5 Clinical and Mission-Critical Implications

**This finding substantially escalates safety concerns raised by Ramirez et al.** If confirmed in humans, the implications are severe:

1. **Chronic therapeutic Temenon use (β≥0.5) may carry genuine, if currently subclinical, neurodegenerative risk**, particularly with treatment durations extending beyond 1-2 years
2. **Oral health monitoring and aggressive periodontal disease prevention should be considered mandatory** for any patient undergoing sustained Temenon therapy
3. **Interstellar mission crews** maintaining β=0.7 coupling for multi-year transit—already flagged by Ramirez et al. for peripheral dysbiosis risk—face a compounded, previously unrecognized CNS risk profile. Given typical mission durations (2-4 years for Ceres-Mars-outer system transit) [10], our 180-day extended-exposure findings, if extrapolated, raise the possibility of clinically significant neurodegenerative burden by mission end—though we stress this extrapolation requires direct longer-duration validation (see Limitations)
4. **Prophylactic strategies** merit urgent investigation: intensive periodontal care protocols, targeted anti-*Porphyromonas* interventions, and the replication-suppressed synbiotic approaches proposed by Ramirez et al. [1] all represent plausible risk-mitigation avenues

### 4.6 Limitations

**1. TAPC identity is provisional.** We characterize this population by surface phenotype and correlative behavior; definitive lineage and functional identity require single-cell transcriptomics and fate-mapping studies not performed here.

**2. 180 days remains a limited duration** relative to proposed multi-year human/mission applications. We cannot confirm that early tau/synuclein changes progress to overt pathology, plateau, or reverse; genuinely long-duration (>1 year) murine studies, though logistically demanding, are needed.

**3. Murine tau/synuclein biology differs from human.** Wild-type C57BL/6J mice do not spontaneously develop human-like tauopathy or synucleinopathy; our findings represent biochemical shifts in endogenous murine protein species, which may not directly predict human disease trajectories. Validation in humanized or transgenic proteinopathy-prone models is an important next step.

**4. Translocation route inference.** While FISH localization to perivascular/periventricular regions is suggestive, we did not directly visualize the translocation event (e.g., live imaging of bacterial crossing at the blood-brain barrier); our data support but do not definitively prove a hematogenous or perivascular translocation route over alternative possibilities (e.g., vagal or trigeminal neural transport, proposed elsewhere for oral pathobionts [5]).

**5. Mediation analysis assumes correctly specified pathway order.** While biologically plausible and consistent with the Ramirez et al. causal experiments, our sequential mediation model cannot fully exclude reverse or bidirectional relationships between some pathway elements (e.g., early neuroinflammation could theoretically influence gut barrier function via neuro-immune feedback, a possibility we did not test).

### 4.7 Priority Directions for Future Work

1. **Single-cell transcriptomic and fate-mapping characterization of TAPCs**
2. **Extended-duration studies (12-24 months)** to determine whether early proteinopathic changes progress to functional/behavioral neurodegenerative phenotypes
3. **Validation in transgenic proteinopathy-prone models** (tau or synuclein overexpression backgrounds) to assess whether Temenon-associated inflammation accelerates disease-relevant pathology
4. **Interventional trials** of periodontal prophylaxis, anti-*Porphyromonas* strategies, and replication-suppressed synbiotics as protective measures
5. **Direct visualization studies** of bacterial translocation route (intravital imaging, blood-brain barrier permeability assays under Temenon exposure)
6. **Human biomarker studies** in existing therapeutic Temenon cohorts, focusing on periodontal status, systemic inflammatory markers, and where ethically feasible, CSF or PET-based tau/synuclein imaging

### 4.8 A Note on Theoretical Completeness

Ramirez et al. proposed that FTB, while locally accurate for eukaryotic host cells, is "systematically incomplete at the organismal level" because it does not account for holobiont dynamics [1]. Our findings extend this concern one level further: **the consequences of that incompleteness are not confined to the gut, but propagate—via a specific, identifiable cellular and molecular pathway—into the brain itself.** Any future revision of Temenon theory aiming for genuine biological completeness must reckon not only with host-microbiome desynchronization, but with the downstream neuroimmune architecture through which that desynchronization becomes disease.

---

## 5. CONCLUSIONS

Chronic Temenon exposure produces measurable microglial activation, oral pathobiont translocation to brain tissue, and early biochemical markers of tau and α-synuclein pathology, substantially mediated by the host-microbiome desynchronization mechanism established by Ramirez et al. (2163). A novel perivascular immune cell population correlates with both translocation burden and proteinopathic markers, offering a candidate cellular intermediary for this process. While our findings remain preliminary with respect to functional or behavioral neurodegenerative outcomes, they represent a coherent, mechanistically tractable, and biologically plausible pathway from a physical intervention (eigentime field exposure) through a microbiological consequence (dysbiosis) to a neuroimmunological one (microglial and perivascular activation) to an early proteinopathic one (tau/synuclein elevation).

This work substantially raises the evidentiary bar that chronic Temenon applications—medical and interstellar alike—must clear before long-duration use can be considered adequately safe. It also demonstrates the scientific value of following a mechanistic thread across organizational levels: from field physics, to cell biology, to microbial ecology, to neuroimmunology. Each step revealed a previously invisible consequence of the step before.

**We altered biological time at the cellular level. The brain, it turns out, was listening the whole time—just not in the way we expected.**
