# The Microbiome-Temenon Paradox: Differential Eigentime Coupling in Prokaryotic-Eukaryotic Symbioses

**Sofia Ramirez¹\*, Amara Chen¹, Miyu Kobayashi², Marcus O'Brien³, Hannah Müller⁴**

**DOI:** 10.2163/symb.chrono.microbiome.014

## ABSTRACT

**Background:** The Fundamental Temenon-Biology (FTB) equation predicts eigentime coupling in cellular systems possessing mitochondrial oscillators. Prokaryotic organisms, lacking mitochondria, were not explicitly considered in the original theoretical framework. Given the obligate symbiotic relationship between mammalian hosts and their microbiota, we hypothesized that differential coupling capacity could produce host-microbiome desynchronization under chronic Temenon exposure.

**Objective:** To determine whether prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells exhibit differential eigentime coupling under identical Temenon field conditions, and to characterize downstream consequences for host-microbiome homeostasis.

**Methods:** We performed 16S rRNA sequencing and metabolomic profiling on gut and oral microbiota from the Chen et al. (2159) murine cohort (archived samples, β=0-0.7 exposure groups). We further generated gnotobiotic mice colonized with defined bacterial consortia and exposed them to calibrated Temenon fields. In vitro experiments assessed bacterial replication kinetics (*Escherichia coli*, *Bacteroides fragilis*, *Porphyromonas gingivalis*) under direct field exposure using modified TEM-Gen-Micro chambers.

**Results:** Bacterial replication rates showed no significant coupling to Temenon fields in vitro (β_eff,bacteria = 0.02±0.04, statistically indistinguishable from zero across all tested β settings). In vivo, chronic exposure (β≥0.5, 90 days) produced significant gut dysbiosis (Shannon diversity −31%, p<0.001) with Proteobacteria expansion (+118%, p<0.001) and reduction in short-chain fatty acid producers. Oral microbiome showed parallel shifts, with *Porphyromonas* and *Fusobacterium* enrichment consistent with periodontal pathobiont profiles. Serum LPS-binding protein and fecal calprotectin were elevated in a dose-dependent manner (r=0.61 with β, p<0.001), correlating with the previously unexplained corticosterone elevation reported in Chen et al. (2159). Gnotobiotic mice colonized with a temporally "locked" (non-replicating, irradiated) bacterial consortium showed attenuated dysbiosis markers despite identical Temenon exposure, supporting a replication-rate-dependent mechanism.

**Conclusions:** Prokaryotic cells do not measurably couple to Temenon fields, creating a fundamental temporal mismatch with eukaryotic host tissue under chronic exposure. This host-microbiome desynchronization offers a parsimonious mechanistic explanation for previously unexplained inflammatory and behavioral findings in Temenon-exposed animals, and raises significant concerns for long-duration human Temenon therapy and interstellar mission applications.

**Keywords:** Temenon field, microbiome, dysbiosis, eigentime coupling, host-symbiont desynchronization, prokaryotic biology, neuroinflammation

## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 The Unexplained Stress Response

Chen et al. (2159) provided the first rigorous multi-site validation of Temenon effects in a mammalian model, confirming circadian period lengthening and telomere stabilization predicted by the FTB equation [1,2]. However, that study also identified an unexpected and mechanistically unexplained finding: progressive corticosterone elevation and anxiety-like behavior emerging at β≥0.5, with no basis in existing theory [1].

The authors explicitly noted this gap: *"chronic corticosterone elevation emerged at β≥0.5... This was NOT predicted by FTB theory"* [1]. Three mechanistic hypotheses were proposed—desynchronization stress, metabolic cost of coupling, and neurobiological specificity—but none were tested directly.

We propose a fourth, previously unconsidered explanation: **host-microbiome temporal mismatch**.

### 1.2 A Gap in the Original Theory

The FTB equation was derived explicitly from mitochondrial oscillator dynamics [3]:

> "Mitochondrien als 'Zeitgeber': Temenon synchronisiert die ATP-Produktionsrate mit τ_T" [3]

This mechanistic assumption is biologically sound for eukaryotic cells, which universally possess mitochondria. However, the mammalian microbiome comprises 10¹³–10¹⁴ bacterial cells [4], the overwhelming majority of which are prokaryotic and **lack mitochondria entirely**. If Temenon coupling operates through mitochondrial resonance, as FTB implies, prokaryotic cells may be **fundamentally incapable of eigentime coupling**, regardless of field strength.

This possibility was not addressed in the original theoretical work, in the K-1 validation protocol, or in subsequent clinical literature [1,3,5,6]. To our knowledge, no published study has measured bacterial response to Temenon fields directly.

### 1.3 Why This Matters

The human/murine host is not a closed biological system—it is an obligate holobiont, dependent on microbial partners for immune development, metabolic function, and (increasingly recognized) neurological health [7,8]. Recent work has identified previously uncharacterized immune cell populations at the brain-periphery interface that appear responsive to systemic inflammatory state [9], and independent lines of evidence link oral microbiome disruption to neurodegenerative risk via periodontal-pathogen-mediated neuroinflammation [10].

If Temenon exposure selectively alters host cell eigentime while leaving bacterial replication kinetics unchanged, the resulting mismatch could plausibly:

1. Disrupt immune tolerance mechanisms calibrated to "normal" microbial turnover rates
2. Create dysbiosis through altered host-derived selective pressures (mucin production, antimicrobial peptides, IgA secretion—all processes with circadian and cell-cycle dependencies)
3. Generate chronic low-grade inflammation independent of any direct neurological effect of Temenon fields
4. Explain the "psychological" stress markers reported in Chen et al. as **downstream inflammatory consequences** rather than primary CNS effects

### 1.4 Study Objectives

1. Determine whether prokaryotic replication kinetics couple to Temenon fields in vitro
2. Characterize microbiome community structure changes under chronic in vivo Temenon exposure
3. Test whether temporally "locked" (non-replicating) bacterial consortia are protected from dysbiosis under identical field conditions
4. Assess correlation between microbiome disruption markers and previously reported stress/behavioral phenotypes

## 2. METHODS

### 2.1 Ethical Approval

This study was approved by the Luna Animal Care Committee (LACC-2161-078), in continuation of protocols established under LACC-2156-042 (Chen et al., 2159). Gnotobiotic animal work received additional approval from the Luna Gnotobiotic Facility Oversight Board (LGFOB-2161-019).

### 2.2 Study Design Overview

This study comprised three complementary experimental arms:

**Arm 1 (Retrospective):** Archived fecal and oral samples from the Chen et al. (2159) cohort (n=221 completers), collected at Day 90, previously frozen at −80°C but not analyzed for microbiome composition in the original study.

**Arm 2 (Prospective, in vitro):** Direct bacterial exposure to calibrated Temenon fields.

**Arm 3 (Prospective, in vivo):** Novel gnotobiotic mouse cohort with defined, temporally manipulable bacterial consortia.

### 2.3 Arm 1: Retrospective Microbiome Analysis

**Samples:** Fecal pellets (n=221) and oral swabs (n=198, subset with adequate sample volume) from Chen et al. cohort, stratified by exposure group (Control, β=0.3, 0.5, 0.7).

**16S rRNA Sequencing:**
- V3-V4 hypervariable region amplification
- Illumina MiSeq platform (2×300bp paired-end)
- QIIME2 v2162.4 pipeline for taxonomic classification
- SILVA database (v144.2) reference

**Diversity Metrics:**
- Alpha diversity: Shannon index, Chao1 richness
- Beta diversity: Bray-Curtis dissimilarity, PERMANOVA

**Metabolomics:**
- Fecal short-chain fatty acids (SCFA): GC-MS (acetate, propionate, butyrate)
- Serum LPS-binding protein (LBP): ELISA
- Fecal calprotectin: ELISA (inflammation marker)

### 2.4 Arm 2: In Vitro Bacterial Temenon Exposure

**Bacterial strains:**
- *Escherichia coli* K-12 MG1655 (facultative anaerobe, common gut commensal)
- *Bacteroides fragilis* NCTC 9343 (obligate anaerobe, dominant gut phylum)
- *Porphyromonas gingivalis* ATCC 33277 (oral pathobiont, periodontal relevance)

**Exposure system:** Modified TEM-Gen-Micro-III chambers adapted for anaerobic/microaerophilic bacterial culture, maintaining β=0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 (matching in vivo conditions), ΔV=5×10⁵ m²/s² via miniature centrifuge integration.

**Growth kinetics:**
- Optical density (OD600) measured every 15 minutes over 24h growth curves
- Generation time calculated via logistic growth model fitting
- n=12 independent cultures per condition per strain

**Analysis:** Generation time compared across β conditions using one-way ANOVA. β_eff for bacterial replication calculated by analogy to Chen et al.'s method [1], substituting generation time for circadian period as the coupling readout.

### 2.5 Arm 3: Gnotobiotic Temporal-Lock Experiment

**Rationale:** If dysbiosis results from differential replication kinetics rather than direct bacterial toxicity of Temenon fields, then bacteria rendered incapable of replication (temporally "locked") should be protected from community-level disruption despite continued host eigentime shift.

**Animals:** Germ-free C57BL/6J mice (n=80), colonized at 6 weeks with a defined 12-member synthetic community (Altered Schaedler Flora-derived, expanded panel) [11].

**Groups:**
1. **Live consortium + Control field (β=0):** n=20
2. **Live consortium + Temenon (β=0.7):** n=20
3. **Irradiated consortium** (γ-irradiated, 25 kGy, replication-incompetent but metabolically active 24-48h) **+ Temenon (β=0.7):** n=20, re-inoculated every 48h to maintain metabolic activity without replication
4. **Live consortium + Temenon (β=0.7) + antibiotic-suppressed replication** (sub-inhibitory ciprofloxacin, replication rate reduced ~70%): n=20

**Duration:** 60 days (shorter than Chen et al. due to gnotobiotic husbandry constraints)

**Endpoints:** Community structure (16S), dysbiosis index, serum LBP, corticosterone, elevated plus maze (as per Chen et al. protocol) [1].

### 2.6 Statistical Analysis

**Software:** R v4.9.0 (packages: phyloseq, DESeq2, vegan, lme4)

**Microbiome differential abundance:** DESeq2 with FDR correction (Benjamini-Hochberg)

**Correlation analysis:** Spearman correlation between dysbiosis markers and behavioral/hormonal data from Chen et al. archived dataset

**Mixed models:** Random effects for original study site (Arm 1) or cage (Arms 2-3)

**Pre-registration:** IPTR-2161-0453

## 3. RESULTS

### 3.1 Bacteria Do Not Couple to Temenon Fields In Vitro

**Table 1. Bacterial Generation Time Under Temenon Exposure**

| Strain | β=0 (Control) | β=0.3 | β=0.5 | β=0.7 | ANOVA p |
|--------|---------------|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| *E. coli* K-12 | 21.4±1.2 min | 21.6±1.4 min | 21.1±1.3 min | 21.8±1.5 min | 0.71 |
| *B. fragilis* | 187±14 min | 184±16 min | 191±15 min | 189±17 min | 0.68 |
| *P. gingivalis* | 425±38 min | 431±41 min | 419±36 min | 428±44 min | 0.82 |

**No significant effect of Temenon field on bacterial generation time for any strain at any β level.**

**Calculated β_eff,bacteria:** 0.02±0.04 (pooled across strains and conditions) — statistically indistinguishable from zero (one-sample t-test vs. 0: p=0.34).

**Comparison to eukaryotic coupling:** Chen et al. reported β_eff,mouse = 0.67±0.061 at nominal β=0.7 [1]. The differential is stark: **eukaryotic host cells show robust, if imperfect, coupling; prokaryotic cells show none.**

This finding directly supports the mitochondrial-dependence hypothesis embedded in the original FTB derivation [3] and represents, to our knowledge, the first direct empirical test of this assumption.

This pattern—expansion of *Porphyromonas* and *Fusobacterium* at the expense of commensal *Streptococcus*—closely mirrors dysbiotic signatures independently associated with periodontal disease and, notably, with emerging evidence linking oral pathobionts to neurodegenerative risk via translocation and systemic inflammation [10]. We note this parallel without claiming direct causal equivalence to any specific human condition; it is offered as a striking structural similarity meriting further investigation.

### 3.4 Dysbiosis Correlates with Previously Unexplained Stress Markers

We tested whether microbiome disruption markers correlated with the corticosterone and anxiety-behavior findings reported in Chen et al. (2159), using the same archived cohort.

| Host Marker (from Chen et al.) | vs. Shannon Diversity | vs. Serum LBP | vs. Proteobacteria % |

**Serum LPS-binding protein**, a marker of gut barrier permeability and endotoxemia [14], was strongly correlated with corticosterone elevation (r=0.64) and inversely correlated with anxiolytic behavior (r=−0.52).

**Mediation analysis** (bootstrapped, 5000 iterations) suggested that **58% of the total effect of β on corticosterone was statistically mediated through LBP/dysbiosis markers**, with the remaining 42% representing a direct or otherwise-mediated pathway (95% CI for mediated proportion: 41-73%).

This does not exclude the CNS-intrinsic mechanisms hypothesized by Chen et al., but indicates that gut-derived inflammation is a major, previously unrecognized contributor to the stress phenotype.

### 3.5 Temporally Locked Bacteria Are Protected from Dysbiosis Despite Identical Temenon Exposure

The gnotobiotic experiment (Arm 3) directly tested whether replication-dependence explains the dysbiosis mechanism.

**This is the key mechanistic finding of the study.** Irradiated (replication-incompetent) bacteria, despite residing in hosts with identical β=0.7 eigentime shift, showed **69% reduction in dysbiosis index** relative to live, freely-replicating consortia (p<0.001), and correspondingly attenuated LBP, corticosterone, and behavioral phenotypes. The antibiotic-suppressed group, with intermediate replication capacity, showed an intermediate phenotype—consistent with a **dose-dependent relationship between bacterial replication rate and dysbiosis severity under host eigentime shift.**

This provides direct experimental support for the temporal mismatch hypothesis: **it is not Temenon field exposure per se that disrupts the microbiome, but the differential between host and bacterial time-dependent processes.**

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Principal Findings

This study provides the first direct evidence that:

1. **Bacterial replication kinetics do not couple to Temenon fields** (β_eff,bacteria ≈ 0), consistent with the mitochondria-dependent mechanism proposed in the original FTB derivation [3] but never previously tested
2. **Chronic host eigentime shift produces progressive, dose-dependent gut and oral dysbiosis**, with Proteobacteria expansion and SCFA depletion characteristic of inflammatory dysbiotic states
3. **This dysbiosis substantially mediates (≈58%) the previously unexplained stress phenotype** reported in Chen et al. (2159)
4. **Experimentally uncoupling bacterial replication from the host-microbiome relationship attenuates dysbiosis and downstream stress markers**, directly supporting a replication-rate-mismatch mechanism

### 4.2 Resolving the "Missing Mechanism" in Chen et al. (2159)

Chen et al. explicitly flagged the corticosterone/anxiety findings as unexplained by FTB theory [1]. Our data suggest this apparent anomaly is not a failure of FTB per se, but a consequence of an **unstated boundary condition**: FTB describes eukaryotic (mitochondria-bearing) cell coupling and implicitly assumes a closed biological system. Real organisms are holobionts. Extending the theory to the host-microbiome unit reveals a previously invisible failure mode.

We propose this be termed the **Microbiome-Temenon Paradox**: *a physically successful intervention at the level of individual host cells produces emergent dysfunction at the level of the host-symbiont system, precisely because the intervention is selectively effective.*

### 4.3 Why Prokaryotes Do Not Couple

The absence of measurable β_eff in bacteria is consistent with, but does not definitively prove, mitochondrial mediation of Temenon coupling. Alternative or contributing explanations include:

- **Absence of membrane-potential-based chronometry:** Mitochondrial membrane potential oscillations have been proposed as a candidate resonance substrate for τ_T coupling [3]; bacteria possess analogous but structurally distinct chemiosmotic gradients across the plasma membrane, which may not present compatible resonance characteristics
- **Absence of nuclear-mitochondrial cross-talk:** Eukaryotic circadian and cell-cycle regulation involves extensive nuclear-mitochondrial signaling [15] with no prokaryotic equivalent
- **Fundamentally different scale of cellular complexity:** Bacterial cell cycles may simply lack the regulatory architecture necessary for external eigentime entrainment

We cannot distinguish between these mechanisms with the present data and flag this as a priority for future biophysical investigation (see Section 4.6).

### 4.4 Clinical and Mission-Critical Implications

**For therapeutic Temenon use:**
Our findings suggest gut and oral microbiome monitoring should become **standard of care** for any patient undergoing sustained Temenon therapy at β≥0.5. Given the mediating role of dysbiosis in the stress phenotype, prophylactic strategies—prebiotic/probiotic co-administration, dietary fiber supplementation to support SCFA production, or even the deliberate use of **replication-suppressed synbiotic formulations** (analogous to our irradiated-consortium protection)—merit clinical trial investigation.

**For interstellar mission applications:**
This finding is particularly concerning for the TEM-Gen-II-equipped vessels now in routine use for multi-year interplanetary transit [16]. Crew members maintaining β=0.7 coupling for mission durations of months to years would, by extrapolation of our 90-day murine data, be expected to develop progressive, potentially severe dysbiosis with attendant systemic inflammation, gut barrier compromise, and secondary neuropsychiatric consequences. We are not aware of systematic microbiome monitoring in current crewed Temenon-equipped missions and recommend this be urgently addressed by mission medical protocols.

**For long-term neurological safety:**
The parallel between our oral pathobiont findings and the recently characterized populations of neuroimmune cells implicated in age-related neurodegeneration [9,10] raises a hypothesis we have not directly tested here but consider urgent: **chronic Temenon-associated oral dysbiosis could constitute a novel, iatrogenic risk factor for accelerated neurodegenerative disease**, operating through the same translocation and neuroinflammatory pathways increasingly implicated in idiopathic neurodegeneration. This requires dedicated longitudinal investigation before broader Temenon deployment in aging or at-risk populations.

### 4.5 Limitations

**1. Retrospective component (Arm 1)**
Archived samples were not collected with microbiome analysis as a pre-specified endpoint in the original Chen et al. protocol; while sample handling was consistent, some degradation cannot be excluded.

**2. Murine-to-human translation**
Mouse and human microbiomes differ substantially in composition [17]; the specific taxa affected (and their human physiological correlates) require independent human validation.

**3. Single defined consortium in gnotobiotic arm**
The 12-member synthetic community, while well-characterized, does not capture the full complexity of a conventional microbiome; effect sizes in conventionally-raised animals may differ.

**4. Mechanism of bacterial non-coupling remains inferential**
We demonstrated absence of coupling but did not directly test candidate biophysical mechanisms (membrane potential oscillation, etc.).

**5. Causality direction in mediation analysis**
While the irradiation experiment provides strong causal support for the replication-dependent mechanism, the retrospective correlational data (Section 3.4) cannot alone establish directionality.

### 4.6 Priority Directions for Future Work

1. **Direct biophysical characterization** of why prokaryotic cells fail to couple (membrane potential imaging under Temenon exposure)
2. **Human microbiome monitoring** in existing therapeutic Temenon cohorts (retrospective sample analysis where available)
3. **Longitudinal neuroinflammatory outcomes** in chronically Temenon-exposed animals, extending beyond 90 days, with direct microglial and neuropathological endpoints
4. **Intervention trials**: prebiotic/probiotic co-therapy, replication-suppressed synbiotics, or engineered "Temenon-coupled" bacterial strains (if biophysically achievable) as protective strategies
5. **Revision of FTB theoretical framework** to explicitly incorporate holobiont-level dynamics rather than isolated host-cell coupling

### 4.7 Theoretical Implications for Temenon Physics

We echo and extend Chen et al.'s conclusion that "life does not follow equations as precisely as we assumed" [1]. Our data suggest a further refinement: **the FTB equation may be locally accurate for individual eukaryotic cells while being systematically incomplete at the organismal level**, because organisms are not closed systems. Any biological application of Temenon technology must account for the **holobiont**, not merely the host genome, as the relevant unit of biological time.

This has a striking parallel to Hayashi-Okonkwo's own unresolved question regarding τ_T's non-global character [3]: just as the field itself cannot be assumed globally coherent across space, host biology cannot be assumed temporally coherent across its symbiotic constituents. **Eigentime homogenization, it turns out, homogenizes selectively—and that selectivity has consequences.**

## 5. CONCLUSIONS

Prokaryotic organisms do not measurably couple to Temenon fields, in direct contrast to the robust (if variable) coupling observed in host eukaryotic tissue. This asymmetry produces a progressive, dose-dependent disruption of gut and oral microbiome communities under chronic Temenon exposure, characterized by reduced diversity, pathobiont expansion, and loss of beneficial metabolic function. We provide direct causal evidence, via a temporal-lock gnotobiotic experiment, that this dysbiosis arises specifically from the differential in replication-dependent processes between host and microbiome—not from direct bacterial toxicity of the field itself.

Critically, this microbiome disruption substantially explains the previously mysterious stress and anxiety phenotypes reported in prior validation work, resolving what had appeared to be an unpredicted side effect into a mechanistically coherent consequence of holobiont-level physiology.

These findings carry immediate implications for clinical Temenon therapy, interstellar mission medicine, and the theoretical completeness of the FTB framework itself. We recommend that microbiome status be incorporated as a standard safety parameter in all future Temenon applications, and that the field's theoretical foundations be extended to explicitly address multi-organism biological time.

**The host is never alone in time. Any technology that forgets this will eventually be reminded.**
